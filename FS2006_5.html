All of these facts can be understood using a scaling law

Scaling Laws

Galileo (yes that one) wrote the square-cube law:

$$A_2 = A_1(\frac{l_2}{l_1})^2$$
and $$V_2 = V_1(\frac{l_2}{l_1})^3$$

If materials are equivalent at both scales:
$\color{orange}{strength \propto cross-section; \ weight \propto \ volume}$

Consequences

An increase in size $[L]$ of any magnitude corresponds to an increase in effective weight $[L^3]$ that is the cube of that magnitude, but only a squared increase in area $[L^2]$.



Strength and transfer properties do not scale well

Small (s) predicts big(b) if . . . .


  • Geometric similarity ($L_{s1}/L_{s2} = L_{b1}/L_{b2}$)

  • Kinematic similarity ($v_{s1}/v_{s2} = v_{b1}/v_{b1})$

  • Dynamic similarity ($F_{s1}/F_{s2} = F_{b1}/F_{b2}$)


Two systems have similtude if they share these similarities
